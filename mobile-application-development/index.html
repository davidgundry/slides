Simplicity

Nielsen (1999) proposes that designers go through all of their design elements and remove them one by one. If a design works just as well without an element, then remove it.

Anti-pattern: Crowded Layouts

It is tempting to cram too much into a screenful of space, making it unwieldy for people to find what they are interested in.

Signal to Noise Ratio

Minimise the amount of unnecessary information presented to the user.

Less information \( \rightarrow \) lower cognitive load

Regularity

Use a regular pattern and limit inessential variation among elements

White space

Allow content space to breathe

Anti-pattern: Button-per-feature

Avoid the temptation of simply adding a new button, menu, or UI feature for every peice of functionality.

Double Duty

Make elements play multiple roles in a design

What roles do the elements to the right serve?

Minimalism

Unnecessary graphics, shading, and decoration can be stripped, leaving a cleaner, crisper, and easier-to-use app.

However, 'flat' UI takes this too far when it becomes hard to use

Anti-pattern: No graphic design

A plain app with just lists of text and a few buttons may not be as appealing.

"The design is not just what it looks like and feels like. The design is how it works" - Steve Jobs

Aesthetics

A certain amount of graphics, colouring and formatting can make an app aesthetically pleasing and enjoyable to use.

The key is achieving both aesthetic appeal and usability

Alignment

Align elements to create visual connections and unity

Nothing should be placed arbitrarily

  • Columns
  • Grids
  • Guide lines

Proximity

Group related elements, put unrelated things far apart

Gestalt Principle of Proximity

We percieve things that are grouped in space as related to each other

Gestalt Principle of Common Region

We see things in the same container as related
